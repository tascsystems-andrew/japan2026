Day 2 · Your First Full Day

Culture, Fashion & Skyline

Sacred shrines, neon fashion, and views that take your breath away. Today you're diving into the best of Tokyo.

Monday, November 16, 2026
9:00

Meiji Shrine

175 acres of forest in the city center

Start your day with purpose. Walk through the massive wooden torii gate and into Meiji Shrine, a 175-acre forest sanctuary dedicated to Emperor Meiji. It's peaceful, spiritual, and the perfect way to ground yourself after jet lag. The main pathway is lined with towering cryptomeria trees. Write a wish on an ema (wooden plaque) and hang it with thousands of others. Completely free.

Tip: Go early (you're already here) to avoid crowds. The light through the trees is magical in the morning.

10:30

Harajuku: Takeshita Street & Beyond

400 metres of pure chaos and fashion rebellion

From Meiji Shrine, walk down the hill to Takeshita Street — 400 metres of visual overload. Crepe shops, kawaii fashion boutiques, costume stores, vintage vinyl, every imaginable subculture colliding on a single narrow street. It's chaotic, it's touristy, but it's also authentically Harajuku.

After Takeshita, slip into Cat Street (parallel to the main drag). It's cooler, quieter, and packed with independent boutiques, art galleries, coffee shops, and street art. This is where Harajuku's authentic personality lives.

Tip: If you get a crepe, do it on Takeshita. It's a rite of passage. The fruit-filled ones are incredible.

12:00

Lunch on Omotesando

Tokyo's Champs-Élysées

Head to Omotesando, Tokyo's answer to the Champs-Élysées. It's a tree-lined boulevard lined with designer flagships designed by famous architects. Browse Omotesando Hills (a luxury complex), then duck into one of the side-street cafés for lunch. Ramen, soba, tonkatsu, sushi — all excellent, all reasonably priced compared to tourist areas. Sit at a counter if you're alone and you'll probably strike up a conversation with someone.

Solo tip: Counter seating is your friend. You're never eating alone; you're eating solo in the middle of Tokyo. Big difference.

14:00

Free Afternoon: Choose Your Own Adventure

The map is a suggestion

Option A — Museums: Visit the Nezu Museum (stunning traditional art, beautiful garden, ¥1,300). It's small, walkable, and feels like a secret.

Option B — Parks: Yoyogi Park is massive and perfect for people-watching, walking, and sitting by the pond. It's peaceful and social at the same time.

Option C — Neighbourhoods: Take the train to Shimokitazawa (2 stops from Shibuya) — a quirky, eclectic neighbourhood full of vintage shops, tiny bars, live music venues, and vintage clothing. Walk the backstreets and see what calls to you.

Option D — Just Wander: The best discoveries in Tokyo come from turning left instead of right. Pick a train line and see where it takes you.

17:00

Shibuya Sky at Sunset

360° views from 230 metres up

Time this one for golden hour. Shibuya Sky is a stunning observation deck 230 metres up with a 360° open-air rooftop. On a clear November day, you can see Mt. Fuji in the distance. The sunset over Tokyo is unforgettable. Book tickets ahead online (¥2,200). Spend an hour up there. Watch the city light up as darkness falls. This is Tokyo at its most beautiful.

Tip: Book online to skip the queue. Bring a light jacket — it's windy up there.

19:00

Dinner in Ebisu

One stop from Shibuya — locals know this place

Descend from the sky and take one train stop to Ebisu. It's more relaxed than Shibuya, full of locals, wine bars, izakayas, and excellent small restaurants without the tourist crush. Walk the back streets and pick somewhere that looks good. You don't need a reservation — just follow your instincts. The food will be delicious, the prices fair, and you'll be eating alongside salarymen and couples and other solo diners.

Solo tip: Tokyo rewards wandering. If something catches your eye — a side street, a shop, a smell, a laugh coming from a bar — follow it. The best moments aren't on any map.
