Suggested Route

A Full Day in Kyoto

You stayed overnight at the Oriental Hotel Kyoto Rokujo while the family headed back to Osaka. Today is all yours — no schedule, no kids, no obligations. Here's a suggested route through some of Kyoto's best hits, but feel free to shuffle, swap, or throw it all out and wander. It's your day.

Getting around: Kyoto is a bus-and-train city. A 1-day bus pass (¥700) is available from your hotel front desk or the bus terminal. Google Maps is your best friend for routes — just type the destination name in English and it'll give you the right bus number. Taxis are also easy to flag down if you'd rather not figure out the buses.
8:00

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove

Ukyo Ward · Free · Open 24 hours

If you're up for an early start, the bamboo grove is magical in the morning before the crowds arrive. The towering stalks filter the light in a way that photographs can't capture. The main path loops through the grove and down to the Togetsukyo Bridge for river views.

Tip: If you go, aim for early — by 10am the paths are shoulder-to-shoulder. 8am is golden hour.

🚌 Bus #28 or JR Sagano Line → Kyoto Station area (~40 min). Then Bus #101 or #205 to Kinkaku-ji (~30 min).
10:00

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)

Kita Ward · ¥500 · 9:00–17:00

The most photographed building in Kyoto — a three-story golden pavilion reflected in a mirror-still pond. It's touristy for a reason. The grounds are beautiful even beyond the main view, with a moss garden and tea house where you can grab matcha and a sweet.

Tip: Your admission "ticket" is actually a paper charm (ofuda) — keep it as a souvenir.

🚌 Bus #12 or #205 to Shijo-Kawaramachi area (~30 min)
12:00

Nishiki Market

Nakagyo Ward · Free to browse · ~10:00–17:00 (varies by stall)

Kyoto's 400-year-old covered market — five blocks of food stalls, pickles, fresh seafood, knives, sweets, and samples. Great for a graze-lunch — skip the sit-down restaurant and just wander from stall to stall. The tamagoyaki (sweet rolled omelette) and fresh mochi are highlights.

Tip: The market runs one block north of Shijo-dori, between Teramachi and Takakura. Look for the arched entrance.

🚶 Walk south to Shijo Station → Keihan Line to Chushojima Station (~25 min total). Or taxi (~20 min, ~¥2,000).
14:30

Gekkeikan Ōkura Sake Museum

Fushimi Ward · ¥600 (includes 3 sake tastings) · 9:30–16:30

If you enjoy sake (or are curious about it), Fushimi is one of Japan's top three brewing regions, and Gekkeikan has been making sake here since 1637. The museum is housed in a renovated 1909 brewery — traditional cedar fermentation tanks, centuries-old brewing tools, and the story of how this neighbourhood became synonymous with sake.

Admission is ¥600 and includes 3 tasting tokens — choose from 8 different sakes plus a plum wine option. Extra tokens are just ¥100 each. If you visit in November, look for the fresh green sugidama (cedar ball) hanging at the brewery entrance — it signals the year's new sake has just been brewed.

Tip: After the museum, take a short walk along the Horikawa canal — old willow trees, white-walled breweries, and the prettiest light in Fushimi.

🚶 Optional 10-min walk to Fushimi Inari (Keihan Fushimi-Inari Station). Or head back to Kyoto Station.
16:00

Fushimi Inari (Optional Bonus)

Fushimi Ward · Free · Open 24 hours

If you've got energy left, the famous 10,000 torii gates are right nearby. You don't need to hike the full mountain — even the first 10 minutes up the trail gives you the iconic tunnel-of-gates photo. Late afternoon light is beautiful here and the crowds start to thin.

Tip: If you're running low on energy, skip this and head back to the hotel for a rest before dinner — no shame in it. But if you've got legs left, even 10 minutes up the trail is worth it.

Open in Maps
🚃 JR Nara Line from Inari Station → Kyoto Station (2 stops, 5 min). Walk or taxi to hotel.
18:00

Dinner in Kyoto

Your choice

You're staying one more night at the Oriental Hotel Rokujo, so take your pick for dinner. The Kyoto Station building has a whole ramen floor (10th floor — Kyoto Ramen Koji) with eight different shops. For something more atmospheric, consider a taxi up to Pontocho alley — a narrow lantern-lit street along the Kamogawa river lined with tiny restaurants.

Tip: November is Kyoto's peak autumn season — some temples offer evening illumination events. Check if Kiyomizu-dera or Eikando are doing night light-ups while you're there.
