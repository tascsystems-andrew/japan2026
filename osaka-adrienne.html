Osaka with Adrienne

Adrienne's first day in Osaka — vintage shops, a takoyaki-making class, retro neon, and the full Dotonbori experience after dark.

Friday, November 20
← D&C Tour ← Adrienne's Page Nov 20
Day 16

Welcome to the Kitchen of Japan

Adrienne arrived from Tokyo last night on the Green Car shinkansen — just in time for Sam's birthday dinner. Today is her real introduction to Osaka, and we're doing it right. Vintage shopping in the morning, a hands-on takoyaki-making class at lunch, the wonderfully weird Shinsekai neighbourhood in the afternoon, and the full neon spectacle of Dotonbori after dark. This is the day Osaka shows off.

The crew: Family + D&C + Adrienne — everyone together for the first time. D&C have already done the castle and Dotonbori, but Dotonbori at night hits different on a second pass, and the rest of today is all new territory.
10:00

Amerikamura

Chuo Ward · Free · Shops open ~11:00

Osaka's answer to Harajuku — a compact neighbourhood of vintage clothing stores, independent boutiques, record shops, street art, and coffee shops built around a small triangle park. It's the youth culture hub of the city, grittier and more fun than Tokyo's version.

The vibe is thrift stores next to skate shops next to tiny cafés. For Adrienne coming from four days in Tokyo, this is a completely different energy — less polished, more personality. The Big Step shopping centre anchors the area and has a good mix of local brands.

🧒 Kids love: The colourful murals and street art, the gachapon machines on every corner, and the general energy of a neighbourhood full of interesting-looking people and interesting-looking things.

Tip: The vintage shops are small and packed — strollers might need to stay outside. Triangle Park is a good kid-corralling spot while adults browse nearby shops.

12:00

Takoyaki-Making Class

Namba / Dotonbori area · Reservation required

The main event of the day — everyone makes their own takoyaki (Osaka's signature octopus balls). A local instructor walks you through the whole process: batter, octopus, toppings, and the crucial wrist-flick that turns them in the mould. You eat what you make. It's messy, fun, and the kind of hands-on experience the kids and adults will all talk about later.

Several places in the Namba/Dotonbori area run these classes in English. We'll book one that accommodates the full group — usually about 90 minutes from start to eating. Lunch is built into this — you'll be full of takoyaki by the end.

🧒 Kids love: Pouring the batter, choosing their toppings, the challenge of flipping the balls with chopsticks (harder than it looks!), and the pride of eating something they actually made. This will be one of their favourite memories of the trip.

Tip: We'll book this in advance — popular classes fill up, especially on weekends. Options include Sakura Cook, Eat Osaka, and several others near Dotonbori.

🚃 Metro from Namba → Dobutsuen-mae (~5 min) or walk south (~15 min) to Shinsekai
14:00

Shinsekai

Naniwa Ward · Free · All day neighbourhood

Osaka's most eccentric neighbourhood — a retro entertainment district built in 1912, modelled half on New York and half on Paris. Today it's a chaotic, neon-lit tangle of giant puffer fish signs, kushikatsu restaurants, old-school arcades, and the Tsutenkaku Tower looming over it all.

D&C saw this on the Nov 7 walking tour, but Adrienne hasn't — and it's the kind of place that's better with a bigger group anyway. Grab some kushikatsu (deep-fried skewers) if you have any room left after takoyaki, hit an arcade, and rub the Billiken statue's feet for good luck.

🧒 Kids love: The insane signage (giant crabs, puffer fish, sumo wrestlers), the arcades (a few rounds of claw machines and racing games), and the Billiken statue at the top of Tsutenkaku Tower.

Tip: Kushikatsu golden rule — dip once in the communal sauce. No double dipping. They take this extremely seriously.

16:00

Afternoon Break

Airbnb · Nishikujo

Recharge before the main event. Kids nap, adults decompress, Adrienne gets a minute to process the fact that she's now in a completely different Japan than Tokyo. The evening session is the non-negotiable — everyone needs to be awake and hungry for it.

17:30

Dotonbori

Chuo Ward · Free · Best after dark

The beating heart of Osaka — and Adrienne's first time seeing it. A canal lined with massive neon signs, the running Glico Man, the mechanical crab, packed with people and the best street food in Japan. This is Osaka at full volume, and it hits completely different when you're seeing it for the first time.

Even for D&C on their second visit, Dotonbori after dark is a different animal. Walk the canal strip, cross the bridges, eat everything — gyoza, yakitori, more takoyaki (you're experts now), okonomiyaki. Finish at Hozenji Yokocho, the tiny lantern-lit alley with the moss-covered shrine, for a quiet contrast to the neon chaos.

🧒 Kids love: The sensory overload — neon everywhere, the Glico Man, street food they can pick and choose, and showing Adrienne everything they already know about Osaka like little tour guides.

Tip: Walk to Ebisubashi Bridge for the classic Glico Man group photo. This is the one everyone needs.

Home

Nishikujo Airbnb

Full bellies, phone full of neon photos, and Adrienne officially inducted into Osaka life. Tomorrow is Uji — a completely different pace. Tonight, sleep well.

🍢 Why the Takoyaki Class Matters

This isn't just a cooking class — it's the group activity for the trip. Everyone's been in Japan for over two weeks by now, the kids are comfortable, D&C are in their groove, and Adrienne just arrived fresh from Tokyo. Making takoyaki together is the kind of chaotic, messy, funny shared experience that bonds a group. The kids will be terrible at flipping them. The adults won't be much better. Everyone eats their own creations. It's perfect.
