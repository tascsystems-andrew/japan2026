Day 3

The Wow Tour

This is the day Osaka makes its first impression — and Osaka doesn't do subtle first impressions. We'll start with the city's most iconic landmark, eat our way through its most famous market, wander the retro streets of its most eccentric neighbourhood, and finish under the neon glow of Dotonbori. By tonight, you'll understand why they call Osaka "Japan's kitchen."

Pace note: This is a full day, but it's built with kid energy in mind. Lots of outdoor walking, frequent snack stops, and nothing that requires silence or standing still for long. If energy drops, we can skip or shuffle — the evening in Dotonbori is the one non-negotiable.
9:30

Osaka Castle Park

Chuo Ward · Castle grounds free, tower ¥600 adults · 9:00–17:00

The morning starts big. Osaka Castle sits on a massive stone-walled plateau surrounded by moats and parkland — it's the most dramatic thing you'll see all trip. The grounds are free and enormous, with wide paths perfect for strollers and kids running ahead. The castle tower is a museum inside (elevator available), and the top floor has panoramic views of the entire city.

The stone walls alone are worth the visit — some of the individual stones are the size of a car, quarried and placed by hand 400 years ago.

🧒 Kids love: The enormous moat (look for turtles), the wide open lawns to run on, the elevator ride up the castle tower, and spotting the golden fish ornaments on the roof.

Tip: Enter from the south side (Tanimachi-yonchome Station) for the most dramatic approach — you cross the moat bridge with the castle framed ahead of you.

11:30

Kuromon Market

Nippombashi · Free entry · Most stalls 9:00–17:00

Known as "Osaka's Kitchen" — a 600-metre covered market that's been running since the 1800s. This is where Osaka's chefs buy their ingredients, and where you should eat lunch. Stall by stall, bite by bite. Grilled scallops on a stick, fresh sashimi, tamagoyaki (sweet rolled omelette), mochi, fruit on sticks, and whatever else looks good.

The market is covered and sheltered, so weather doesn't matter. It's narrow in spots but manageable with a stroller.

🧒 Kids love: Eating with their hands, choosing what looks interesting, the tamagoyaki stalls (sweet and familiar), fruit skewers, and the sheer volume of things to point at and try.

Tip: Come hungry. Don't eat a big breakfast before this — the whole point is to graze your way through.

13:30

Shinsekai

Naniwa Ward · Free · All day neighbourhood

Osaka's most eccentric neighbourhood — built in 1912 as a "new world" entertainment district, modelled half on New York and half on Paris. Today it's a wonderfully retro, slightly chaotic tangle of neon signs, puffer fish lanterns, arcades, and kushikatsu restaurants.

Tsutenkaku Tower sits in the middle of it all — a 1950s observation tower with views of the neighbourhood and a lucky Billiken statue at the top. It's kitschy, old-school, and very Osaka.

🧒 Kids love: The crazy signage (giant puffer fish, crabs, sumo wrestlers), popping into an arcade for a few rounds, and rubbing the Billiken statue's feet for good luck at the top of the tower.

Tip: Kushikatsu rule — never double dip in the communal sauce. Dip once, eat, done. They take this very seriously.

17:30

Dotonbori

Chuo Ward · Free · Best after dark

The beating heart of Osaka — and the image you've seen on every Japan travel poster. A canal lined with massive neon signs (the running Glico Man, the mechanical crab, the gyoza-eating dragon), packed with people, noise, and the best street food in the country. This is Osaka at full volume.

Walk the main strip along the canal, cross the bridges, and eat everything. Takoyaki from a street stall, okonomiyaki from a hole-in-the-wall, gyoza, yakitori — it's an all-you-can-eat buffet disguised as a neighbourhood.

🧒 Kids love: The sheer sensory overload — neon everywhere, the running Glico Man, the mechanical crab, choosing takoyaki toppings, and just the energy of a place that feels like a carnival every single night.

Tip: Walk to Ebisubashi Bridge for the classic Glico Man photo. Then duck one block south into Hozenji Yokocho — a tiny lantern-lit alley with a moss-covered shrine. A beautiful contrast to the neon chaos.

🍢 Osaka Street Food — The Essentials

Takoyaki — Crispy-outside, molten-inside octopus balls. Topped with sauce, mayo, bonito flakes, and green onion. Osaka's #1 street food.

Okonomiyaki — A thick savoury pancake made with batter, cabbage, and your choice of filling. Cooked on a griddle, slathered in sauce. "Okonomi" means "as you like it."

Kushikatsu — Deep-fried skewers of everything — meat, shrimp, vegetables, cheese, even mochi. Dipped once in communal sauce. No double dipping.

Gyoza — Pan-fried dumplings. Osaka-style are thin-skinned and crispy on the bottom.

Tamagoyaki — Sweet rolled omelette, often on a stick at markets. Kids love this one.
