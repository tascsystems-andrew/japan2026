Day 3

Into the Forest, Then Into the Water

Today we leave Hamamatsu city and head west to the Kanzanji area on the shores of Lake Hamana. The morning is a real-life Ghibli village. The afternoon and evening are for soaking in hot springs and eating a kaiseki dinner at a traditional ryokan. This is the day everyone will remember.

9:00

Check Out — Richmond Hotel

Pack up and head west

Check out of the hotel and grab a taxi to the Kanzanji area (~30 min from Hamamatsu Station). Everything today is within a few minutes of each other once you're out there.

🚕 Taxi from Hamamatsu Station to Nukumori no Mori (~30 min, ~¥5,000–6,000). Or Entetsu Bus #30 (~40 min, much cheaper but slower with luggage.
10:00

Nukumori no Mori

Kanzanji area · ¥600 adults, free elementary age & under · 10:00–17:00 · Closed Thursdays

A tiny fairytale village in the forest, built over 30 years by architect Shigeyoshi Sasaki. Whimsical mushroom-shaped cottages, winding stone paths, a gelato shop, a cheesecake workshop, an owl café, and shops selling handmade pottery, leather goods, and jewellery. It feels like stepping into a Studio Ghibli film.

The whole village is about 2,400 square metres — small enough to see everything, but charming enough that you'll want to linger. Plan about 2 hours.

🧒 Kids love: The mushroom houses (some are small enough for only them to fit inside), the owl café, the gelato, and the general feeling of being in a real-life fairy tale. Every corner is photo-worthy.

Tip: Bring cash — some shops and the entrance are cash or PayPay only.

Open in Maps
🚕 Short taxi to the ryokan (~5–10 min). Everything in the Kanzanji area is close together.
13:00

Check In — Sansuikan Kinryu

Kanzanji Onsen · 2 rooms · Kaiseki dinner included

Your ryokan for the night — a traditional Japanese hot spring inn on Lake Hamana. Check in, change into the provided yukata robes, and settle in. The rest of the day is for relaxing.

PM

Hot Springs & Relaxation

Kanzanji Onsen · In-house

Soak in the onsen (hot spring baths). Most ryokans have both indoor and outdoor baths, with views of the lake. The hot mineral water is incredibly relaxing after days of walking and train rides. Take your time — this is what Japan does better than anywhere in the world.

🧒 Kids love: Wearing yukata robes around the ryokan (they'll feel like little samurai), the novelty of communal baths, and the tatami mat rooms where everyone sleeps on futons on the floor.
18:00

Kaiseki Dinner

Included with your stay

A multi-course traditional Japanese dinner served in your room or in the ryokan's dining hall. Expect beautifully presented small dishes — seasonal sashimi, grilled fish, tempura, tofu, pickled vegetables, miso soup, and rice. Each course is a little work of art. This is one of the most memorable dining experiences in Japan.

🧒 Kids love: The sheer number of little dishes (it feels like a feast), the novelty of eating in a tatami room, and there's usually rice and simple items even picky eaters will enjoy.

Tip: Let the ryokan know in advance if you have any dietary needs or allergies — they're usually very accommodating.

🧖 Onsen Etiquette — Quick Guide

Before the bath: Rinse thoroughly at the washing stations before entering the water. This is non-negotiable — it's the #1 rule.

Towels: Small towels can come with you (keep it on your head, not in the water). Large towels stay in the changing room.

Tattoos: Some onsen don't allow tattoos. Sansuikan Kinryu should be family-friendly, but if concerned, ask at check-in about private bath options.

With kids: Young children are welcome in onsen. Same-gender parent accompanies. Keep them calm and remind them not to splash or swim.
