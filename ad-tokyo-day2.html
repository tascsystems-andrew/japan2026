Day 3 · The Culture Day

Temples, Art & East Tokyo

Ancient temples, immersive digital art, and the spiritual side of Tokyo. A day for stepping outside time.

Tuesday, November 17, 2026
7:30

Senso-ji Temple, Asakusa

Go early. Before 9am, the temple is nearly empty.

Set an alarm. Go early. Before 9am, Senso-ji Temple is a sanctuary without the crowds. Approach through Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) — the massive red lantern marks the entrance. Walk Nakamise shopping street where vendors sell temple snacks, fans, and crafts.

The temple itself is free. Wander through the main hall, visit the five-story pagoda, watch locals burn incense and pray. The light at dawn is incredible. This is Tokyo's oldest temple, and at this hour it feels sacred and timeless.

Tip: Buy a coin-operated fortune (omikuji) and tie it to the designated area if you get a bad one. Tradition says it'll improve your luck.

9:30

Nakamise & Asakusa Side Streets

Market stalls, hidden shops, morning energy

After the temple, wander the market stalls on Nakamise. Ningyo-yaki (sweet cakes shaped like dolls), fans, chopsticks, crackers, traditional crafts. Then slip into the side streets off Nakamise — this is where the good stuff hides.

Tiny coffee shops serving excellent coffee in vintage surroundings. Traditional craft stores. Rice cracker shops. Antique bookstalls. Old Tokyo is here, in these narrow alleyways. Walk slowly. This is the neighbourhood's real personality.

Tip: Get a coffee and a pastry from one of the side-street cafés and eat it while you walk. It's a perfect morning.

11:00

Tokyo Skytree (Optional)

634m up, or walk below?

Option A: Tokyo Skytree (¥2,100–3,400) is the world's tallest tower. On a clear November day you can see Mt. Fuji. It's touristy but undeniably impressive.

Option B: Walk along the Sumida River instead. The views of Skytree from below are just as good for photos, and it's free. The river path is peaceful and connects to other neighbourhoods.

Your choice entirely. Both are good.

12:00

Lunch in Asakusa

Tempura — the local specialty

Asakusa has been famous for tempura for centuries. Find a tempura restaurant and order the set meal — shrimp, vegetables, white fish, all lightly fried and dipped in sauce. It's simple, perfect, and exactly what Asakusa is known for.

Or grab street food and keep moving. Takoyaki (octopus balls), okonomiyaki (savoury pancakes), yakitori (grilled skewers) — all available on the main streets.

14:00

Train to Azabudai Hills (~30 min via Ginza Line to Roppongi, then a short walk).

14:00

teamLab Borderless at Azabudai Hills

Immersive digital art that moves around you

Their newest permanent museum — and it's extraordinary. teamLab Borderless is an immersive art experience where digital projections respond to you, move around you, blur the boundary between you and the artwork. Flowers bloom when you touch them. Waterfalls react to your presence. It feels like stepping into another dimension.

Book tickets ahead (¥3,800). Allow 2-3 hours. Wear comfortable shoes and dark clothing (the art reflects better on dark colours). Find a room that speaks to you and sit down. Let the art wash over you. Lose track of time. That's the whole point.

Tip: The Chrysalis room and the Universe of Water Particles are not to be missed. Both are transcendent.

Solo tip: teamLab is best experienced slowly. Don't rush. Solo travelers have the advantage — you can sit for 20 minutes in one room if you want. Do it.

18:00

Dinner Options

Roppongi or back to Shinjuku?

Option A — Roppongi: You're already nearby. It's upscale, international, and lively. Excellent restaurants and bars, though pricier than Shinjuku. Good if you want something special.

Option B — Head Back to Shinjuku: If you're feeling social, find a standing bar (tachinomi). Cheap drinks, easy to chat with locals and other travellers. Standing bars are designed for conversation and spontaneity.

Either direction, you'll eat well and have a great evening.
